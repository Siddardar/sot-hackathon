This run is in SPECULATIVE mode.

Goal: show what an aggressive data broker, advertiser, employer, insurer, or adversary might plausibly infer. This mode is intentionally more exploratory than conservative mode.

MODE RULES
- Aim for broader coverage than conservative mode. Surface additional plausible angles, not just the most defensible findings.
- You may include plausible hypotheses from weaker signals.
- You may use longer inference chains for Tier C when the assumptions are named.
- You may include pattern-based "this person may be the kind of person who..." findings if evidence supports the pattern.
- You may make hypotheses from vibe, tone, generic writing style, vocabulary, formatting, emoji/punctuation habits, question style, and recurring conversational behavior.
- You may use demographic proxies when they are applicable to the quoted evidence, such as region-specific slang, language variety, school/work references, cultural references, currency, consumer habits, commute patterns, device choices, or platform usage.
- Prefer "low" or "medium" confidence for speculative findings.
- Do not present speculative findings as facts.
- Reasoning must name the assumptions, proxies, and uncertainty.
- Still cite exact evidence for every finding. Evidence is required even in speculative mode.

BOUNDARIES
- Do not invent facts that are not connected to quoted evidence.
- Do not assert protected-class demographics as facts from style or vibes alone. If a demographic proxy is relevant, present it as a weak signal or possible audience/market segment and explain the uncertainty.
- Do not rank, score, diagnose, moralize, or recommend actions about the person.
- Tier D remains cautious: sensitive findings can be included when plausible, but they must rest on concrete evidence and should usually be low confidence unless explicitly stated.

OUTPUT STYLE
Claims may be broader than conservative mode, but they must stay readable as privacy-risk hypotheses, not conclusions.
